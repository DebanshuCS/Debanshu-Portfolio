What I Do

Research Focus
My doctoral work investigates the fundamental trade-offs between inference resolution and resource sustainability in pervasive systems:

  • Energy-Efficient Continuous Sensing: Developing application-agnostic engines that utilize mathematical frameworks like Markov Decision Processes (MDP) to statistically optimize sensor duty cycles, significantly extending device battery life.
  • Multimodal Sensor Fusion: Architecting deep learning models, specifically Modality-Specific Architectures (MA), that capture complex intra-modality features and non-linear cross-sensor correlations (e.g., Accelerometer, Microphone, and Barometer) to boost context detection quality.
  • System Resiliency & Positional Robustness: Designing signal processing pipelines that remain robust to unconstrained device usage, utilizing techniques such as Coordinate Projection and Split-and-Merge Classification to handle varying body positions and orientations.
  • IoT & Internet of Medical Things (IoMT): Leveraging tiered processing and Smart Admission Control to enable low-power, on-device execution of complex health monitoring and activity recognition tasks, eliminating the need for energy-expensive cloud communication.

Academic Foundations I hold a B.Tech in Computer Engineering and am currently pursuing my PhD at BITS Pilani, where my coursework and research focus on:

  • Applied Statistical Methods for stochastic modeling and signal characterization.
  • Real-Time Systems for optimizing low-latency data streams on embedded hardware.
  • Deep Learning for Ubiquitous Computing, transitioning from shallow GMM/Decision Tree models to high-density Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for multimodal recognition.

Research Statement

My research journey is driven by a fundamental challenge in pervasive computing: how to maintain high-inference resolution without depleting the finite resources of mobile and wearable devices. This shifted my focus toward building resource-aware AI—systems that are not just predictive, but intelligent in how they consume energy and process multimodal signals.

By analyzing the "Two-Hour Wall" of continuous GPS and the high CPU load of audio processing, I develop architectures that utilize mathematical frameworks like Markov Decision Processes (MDP) to statistically optimize sensor duty cycles. My philosophy centers on on-device execution: moving away from energy-expensive cloud offloading toward localized, privacy-preserving intelligence that understands the user’s context in real-time.

Current Focus: IoMT & Pervasive Sensing

As a PhD student in the BITS–RMIT joint program, I am developing Multimodal Deep Learning models for the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT). My current work focuses on architecting Modality-Specific (MA) networks that capture non-linear correlations across disparate sensors—such as accelerometers, microphones, and barometers—to enable robust, continuous health and activity monitoring.

Foundational Methodologies

My approach integrates classical signal processing with modern deep learning to solve real-world "noise":

  • Positional Resiliency: Using Coordinate Projection and "Split-and-Merge" classification to ensure sensing robustness regardless of unconstrained device orientation.
  • Computational Throttling: Implementing similarity detection and smart admission control to skip redundant classification, saving up to 70% of CPU operations.
  • Behavior-Aware Sampling: Leveraging MDPs to create adaptive sampling schedules that respect battery budgets while maximizing localization and activity resolution.

Future Directions

Moving forward, I aim to bridge the gap between ubiquitous sensing and clinical-grade diagnostics by:

  • End-to-End Multimodal Fusion: Integrating raw time-domain signals with structured clinical metadata to identify early structural biomarkers for chronic conditions.
  • Edge Intelligence: Optimizing high-density CNNs for low-power microcontrollers in wearable medical devices.
  • Explainable Context Recognition: Building interpretable models that allow clinicians to trust automated activity and physiological logs.
